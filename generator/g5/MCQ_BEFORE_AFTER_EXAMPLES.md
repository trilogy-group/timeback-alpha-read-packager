# MCQ Before/After Examples

Eight pairs showing a defective item alongside a corrected version. Each bad item leaks the answer through a structural flaw; each good item requires the student to read and track the passage to respond correctly.

---

## Example 1: WORLD_KNOWLEDGE_KEY

**Passage:**
Plants make their own food through a process called photosynthesis. Inside each leaf are tiny structures called chloroplasts. Chloroplasts contain a green pigment named chlorophyll, which captures energy from sunlight. The plant uses that energy to combine carbon dioxide from the air with water absorbed through its roots. The result is glucose, a sugar the plant uses to grow. Oxygen is released as a byproduct, which is how plants help keep the air breathable for animals and humans.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: WORLD_KNOWLEDGE_KEY
Why leaked: Any fifth-grader who has been told "plants make oxygen" will pick B without reading a word. The answer is retrievable from general science class memory, not from this specific passage. The passage does say it, but so does every science textbook and the student's prior knowledge alone is sufficient to answer correctly.

According to the passage, what does a plant release during photosynthesis?

A. Carbon dioxide
B. Oxygen
C. Nitrogen
D. Glucose

Key: B

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: All four options describe plausible plant-biology mechanisms. Options A and D describe real steps in the process but swap the role of chlorophyll with other parts (roots absorb water; oxygen release is the byproduct step). Option C sounds biochemically plausible even though it is passage-wrong. Only a reader who tracked the specific causal chain "chlorophyll captures sunlight → energy used to combine CO2 + water" can confidently select B.

The passage explains that chlorophyll serves a specific role before glucose can be made. What is that role?

A. It absorbs water from the soil and carries it up to the leaves.
B. It captures energy from sunlight so the plant can combine carbon dioxide and water.
C. It breaks down glucose into smaller sugars the plant can store in its roots.
D. It releases oxygen into the air after the plant has finished making food.

Key: B

## Example 2: ABSOLUTE_LANGUAGE

**Passage:**
The water cycle moves water continuously between Earth's surface and the atmosphere. When the sun heats lakes, rivers, and oceans, liquid water turns into water vapor through evaporation. The vapor rises and cools as it climbs higher in the atmosphere. Cooling causes the vapor to condense into tiny droplets that form clouds. When the droplets combine and grow heavy enough, they fall back to Earth as precipitation — rain, snow, sleet, or hail — and the cycle begins again.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: ABSOLUTE_LANGUAGE
Why leaked: Options A, B, and D each contain absolute words — "always," "never," "never" — which trained test-takers know are almost always wrong in science items. A student can eliminate three options on test-taking strategy alone without reading the passage. This collapses a 4-option item into a 1-option giveaway.

Why does water vapor form clouds, according to the passage?

A. It always rises because water vapor is lighter than air.
B. It never stays at ground level due to wind pressure.
C. It cools as it rises, causing it to condense into tiny droplets.
D. It is never absorbed back into the ocean before reaching the sky.

Key: C

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: Option A reverses the thermal logic (vapor cools, it does not absorb more heat). Option B introduces wind as the mechanism, which is world-plausible and the passage never rules out wind, so a non-reader might pick it. Option D invokes carbon dioxide, a known atmospheric gas, making it passage-wrong but world-plausible. Only the reader who tracked the explicit cause-and-effect chain — rises, then cools, then condenses — can reliably choose C.

According to the passage, what causes water vapor to condense into the droplets that form clouds?

A. The vapor absorbs heat from the sun as it climbs higher in the atmosphere.
B. Wind pushes the vapor together until the droplets are heavy enough to stick.
C. The vapor cools as it rises to higher parts of the atmosphere.
D. The vapor mixes with carbon dioxide, which triggers the change to liquid droplets.

Key: C

## Example 3: LONGEST_OPTION_TELL

**Passage:**
In 1804, Meriwether Lewis and William Clark led an expedition from St. Louis westward to the Pacific Ocean. President Jefferson commissioned the journey to map the newly purchased Louisiana Territory and find a water route to the Pacific. The Corps of Discovery, as the group was called, included about 33 members. A Shoshone woman named Sacagawea joined the expedition and proved invaluable — she helped the group communicate with Native nations, identify edible plants, and navigate unfamiliar terrain.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: LONGEST_OPTION_TELL
Why leaked: Option B is roughly three times the length of every other option and packs in elaborating clauses. Test-wise students learn that item writers pad the correct answer to make it fully defensible. The length asymmetry signals the key before the student evaluates content. Options A, C, and D are each one short phrase.

Why did President Jefferson send Lewis and Clark on their expedition?

A. He wanted them to establish new trading posts along the Mississippi River.
B. He wanted to map the Louisiana Territory and find a water route to the Pacific Ocean, because the United States had recently purchased the territory and needed detailed geographic information about this vast, largely unknown land to understand its resources and boundaries.
C. He needed soldiers to protect settlers moving west.
D. He hoped to find gold deposits in the Rocky Mountains.

Key: B

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: All four options are two-part answers of similar length. Options A and D mix in plausible period-appropriate goals (trade, forts, protection) that Jefferson actually pursued in other contexts but are not stated here. Option C pulls in the Mississippi, a geographically adjacent detail that could seem right. Only passage readers who noted the two explicit purposes — mapping the territory and finding a Pacific water route — can select B confidently.

According to the passage, President Jefferson had two specific goals for the Lewis and Clark expedition. What were they?

A. To protect settlers and to establish trade with Native nations
B. To map the Louisiana Territory and to find a water route to the Pacific
C. To survey the Mississippi River and to locate gold in the Rockies
D. To count the Native nations in the region and to build western forts

Key: B

## Example 4: TOPIC_GENERAL

**Passage:**
The Arctic fox survives winters that would kill most animals. Its thick, layered coat traps body heat so efficiently that the fox does not begin to shiver until temperatures drop below negative 70 degrees Celsius. In autumn, its fur changes from brown to white, which hides the fox against the snow. The fox also has short, rounded ears that reduce heat loss, and fur-covered paws that act like snowshoes, distributing its weight so it can walk on soft snow without sinking.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: TOPIC_GENERAL
Why leaked: Option B — "keeps the fox warm in cold climates" — is a true general statement about any animal's winter coat and is true of the Arctic fox's coat as well. A student who has never read this passage can reason: "coats keep animals warm, so B is true." The item now has two defensible answers (A and B), or a student picks B on world knowledge and gets it wrong. Either way the item fails to measure reading.

How does the Arctic fox's coat help it survive winter?

A. It changes color to help the fox hide from predators in the snow.
B. It keeps the fox warm in cold climates.
C. It makes the fox lighter so it can run faster across the ice.
D. It absorbs sunlight to raise the fox's body temperature.

Key: A

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: Options B and D involve heat/sunlight interactions with fur color — physically plausible mechanisms a student might half-remember from science. Option C invokes animal communication, which is world-plausible for territorial species. None of B, C, or D is supported by the passage. Only A matches the passage's explicit statement that white fur hides the fox against snow.

The passage states that the Arctic fox's coat color changes in autumn. What specific advantage does the passage say this gives the fox?

A. It helps the fox blend into the snow, making it harder to see.
B. It reflects sunlight and keeps the fox cooler during brief warm spells.
C. It signals to other foxes that winter territory boundaries have shifted.
D. It helps the fox absorb more body heat from the pale winter sun.

Key: A

## Example 5: PAIRED_CONTRADICTION

**Passage:**
In Keiko Tanaka's story, twelve-year-old Mia discovers a box of her grandmother's letters hidden in the attic. At first she plans to read just one, to satisfy her curiosity. But each letter reveals a piece of her grandmother's life before immigrating — a music teacher, a small apartment, a city Mia has never visited. Mia stays in the attic for hours, missing dinner entirely. When her mother calls up the stairs, Mia doesn't answer, afraid that answering will mean putting the letters down.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: PAIRED_CONTRADICTION
Why leaked: Options C and D directly contradict each other — C says she is never interested in family history, D says she always disobeys when curious. Both use absolute language and they cannot both be true. A careful reader recognizes the logical contradiction and eliminates both C and D, effectively turning a 4-option item into a 2-option coin flip — one of which, option B, is trivially implausible. The contradiction leaks the key.

Why does Mia stay in the attic for hours instead of coming down for dinner?

A. She is so absorbed in the letters that she loses track of time and does not want to stop reading them.
B. She cannot hear her mother calling because the attic is too far from the kitchen.
C. She is never interested in family history, so she ignores the call.
D. She always disobeys her mother when she is curious about something.

Key: A

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: Option A (embarrassment) is emotionally plausible for a child who missed dinner. Option C (wanting company) is a warm, relatable motivation. Option D (too focused to hear) is physically plausible and a common experience. None is logically impossible; all are things real children do. Only readers who reached the final sentence — "afraid that answering will mean putting the letters down" — have direct textual evidence for B.

Why does Mia not answer when her mother calls up the stairs?

A. She is embarrassed that she missed dinner and does not want to explain herself.
B. She fears that answering will mean she has to put the letters down and stop reading.
C. She is hoping her mother will come upstairs and read the letters with her.
D. She cannot hear her mother because she is too focused on the letters.

Key: B

## Example 6: PASSAGE_IGNORABLE

**Passage:**
Marie Curie was the first person to win two Nobel Prizes, in Physics in 1903 and Chemistry in 1911. Her research focused on radioactivity — a term she herself coined. Working in a converted shed in Paris, she isolated two new elements: polonium, named after her home country of Poland, and radium. Curie's work was dangerous; she had no way of knowing that prolonged exposure to radioactive materials would damage her health. She died in 1934 from aplastic anemia, almost certainly caused by decades of radiation exposure.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: PASSAGE_IGNORABLE
Why leaked: A student who knows nothing about this passage but knows that Marie Curie died from radiation exposure — a famous historical fact — can answer C without reading a word. More broadly, the four options can be ranked by plausibility from general knowledge: C is the famous answer, B is historically true but not the point, A and D are invented. The passage is decorative here; world knowledge drives the ranking.

Why was Curie's research dangerous?

A. Her shed laboratory had no heating and she worked in freezing conditions.
B. She was a woman working in a field dominated by men who opposed her.
C. She was regularly exposed to radioactive materials without knowing they could harm her.
D. She used unstable chemicals that could have exploded at any time.

Key: C

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: All four options are genuine passage details (shed, naming polonium, coining the term, two Nobel Prizes). Options A and D require inference but go in a wrong direction — the shed speaks to resources, not knowledge of risk; the Nobel Prizes speak to recognition, not ignorance of danger. Option B is a non sequitur dressed as reasoning. Only option C ties a specific passage fact (she coined the term, meaning the field was new to science) directly to the "no way of knowing" claim.

The passage says Curie had "no way of knowing" her research would damage her health. What detail in the passage best supports this claim?

A. She worked in a converted shed, which suggests she lacked proper laboratory safety equipment.
B. She named polonium after Poland, showing her focus was on discovery rather than personal safety.
C. She coined the term "radioactivity" herself, indicating the field was so new that its health risks were not yet understood.
D. She won two Nobel Prizes, which shows the scientific community valued her work before its dangers were recognized.

Key: C

## Example 7: STEM_TOO_EASY

**Passage:**
The Grand Canyon stretches 277 miles long and reaches depths of more than a mile. It was formed over millions of years as the Colorado River slowly cut through layers of rock. Different layers record different geological periods — the deeper you go, the older the rock. The canyon's exposed walls act like a history book, allowing scientists to study rock layers that would otherwise be buried far underground. Some of the deepest rocks at the canyon's floor are nearly two billion years old.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: STEM_TOO_EASY
Why leaked: The stem is a near-complete paraphrase of the passage's central explanation. It restates the metaphor AND immediately explains what it means AND specifies the older/deeper relationship. The student does not need to interpret the metaphor or locate evidence — the stem hands them the answer. Any student who can read the stem can select A without consulting the passage at all.

The passage compares the canyon's walls to a history book. This comparison helps the reader understand that the canyon walls show layers of rock that record different time periods in Earth's history, with older rock found deeper in the canyon.

A. True — the walls show layers, and deeper layers are older.
B. False — the walls show only one rock type throughout.
C. False — the deepest rocks are the youngest.
D. False — the canyon was formed by wind, not water.

Key: A

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: Options C and D describe measurement activities at the canyon that sound scientifically plausible. Option A uses the word "impossible" which overstates, but more importantly it misreads what the passage emphasizes — the passage says the walls expose what would "otherwise be buried," not that digging is impossible. The student must connect the history-book metaphor specifically to the idea of reading exposed layers to understand time periods, which is only clear from reading the full explanation.

The passage compares the canyon's walls to a history book. What does this comparison suggest about how scientists use the canyon?

A. They use the canyon as a shortcut to study ancient rock that would otherwise be impossible to reach.
B. They read the canyon's layers to learn about different geological time periods without digging into buried ground.
C. They measure the canyon's depth to calculate how fast the Colorado River flows today.
D. They count the canyon's rock layers to estimate how much water the river carried in the past.

Key: B

## Example 8: NEAR_NEIGHBOUR_GOOD

**Passage:**
Every spring, the Songkran festival marks the Thai New Year with a nationwide water celebration. Families visit temples early in the morning to offer food to monks and pour scented water over Buddha statues as a sign of respect and renewal. Later in the day, the streets fill with people splashing water on one another — a tradition that began as a gentle blessing but has grown into a joyful, days-long event. The water symbolizes washing away bad luck from the previous year to welcome good fortune ahead.

**BAD MCQ (leaked — answerable without reading):**
Defect: NEAR_NEIGHBOUR_GOOD
Why leaked: Note — this item is actually well-constructed and is included here as a contrast example. Option B is plausible (Songkran is in April, Thailand is hot) and a non-reader might pick it. Option C connects water to monks, which is partially in the passage (water is poured over Buddha statues near temples) but misattributes the symbolism. Option D invokes the monsoon, a real Thai weather feature, but the passage never mentions it. Each wrong option is passage-wrong but world-plausible.

According to the passage, what does the water symbolize during Songkran?

A. Washing away bad luck from the previous year to welcome good fortune
B. Cooling people down during the hot Thai spring weather
C. Honoring the monks who live in the local temples
D. Marking the end of the monsoon season in Thailand

Key: A

**GOOD MCQ (requires reading):**
Why requires reading: Option A (government parade → neighborhood custom) is a plausible evolution for a national festival. Option C (children splashing elders → community-wide) follows a believable social diffusion pattern and is the kind of detail that could appear in a passage about a cultural tradition. Option D (temple grounds → streets) is partially supported by the passage's geography but inverts the causal story. None is wild or obviously wrong; all require the reader to have tracked the specific "began as a gentle blessing" phrasing to reject them confidently.

According to the passage, the street water-splashing tradition was not always as large as it is today. What does the passage say about how it changed?

A. It began as a government-organized parade and later became a neighborhood custom.
B. It started as a gentle blessing and grew into a joyful, days-long event.
C. It began with children splashing elders and gradually spread to the whole community.
D. It started inside temple grounds and eventually moved into the surrounding streets.

Key: B
